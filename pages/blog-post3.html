Dictionaries

October 2022

A dictionary is a data structure that contains keys that are mapped to values (key:value pairs), called an associative array. Dictionaries are mutable and can be indexed through their keys, which must be unique and made from immutable datatypes (eg: strings or integers). Dictionaries are unordered, if you iterate through a dictionary the key:value pairs may show up in any order. Dictionaries are represented by curly brakets and key:value pairs separated by commas, eg: 

          
  my_dict = {'Name': 'Matt', 'Year': 2022, 'Grades': [H1, H1, H1]}

Dictionaries are useful for storing data that has a matching association, eg: 'Name':'Matt'. Also, because they use hashing to map the data, dictionaries are very fast to look up - best case O(1) and worse case O(n). An example of using a dictionary is when you want to match names and numbers from a phonebook, because phone numbers are unique identifiers: 

          
  phonebook_dict = {'0432 195 205': 'Dave', '9362 1345': 'Jane'}
